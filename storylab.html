Hook
Grab your reader from the very first word

✨ Writing tips for your hook
  • Start with a bold, surprising statement that makes the reader think "What?!"
  • Or drop us straight into action — something is already happening
  • Or ask a question the reader desperately wants answered
  • 1–3 sentences only. Make every word earn its place.

Example 1 — Action opening
The window shattered before Marcus even heard the sound. Glass exploded inward like a thousand tiny stars, and he threw himself to the floor, heart hammering against the cold tiles.
Example 2 — Bold statement
The last thing Priya expected to find in her grandmother's attic was a door that hadn't existed the day before. But there it was — solid oak, brass handle, and absolutely no explanation.
Example 3 — Question hook
Have you ever had the feeling that someone is watching you? Not the paranoid prickle you get walking home alone — I mean the absolute, bone-deep certainty that somewhere, something knows your name.

Introduction
Who, what, where, when — set the scene
0 words
▼
✨ Writing tips for your introduction
  • Introduce your main character — who are they and what do we need to know?
  • Paint the setting — use your senses (sight, sound, smell, touch, feeling)
  • Set up the normal world before things go wrong
  • Hint at the problem without revealing it — create a sense of unease or wonder
  • Give the reader a reason to care about your character
✦ 3 examples of great writing Click a word to copy it to your writing
Example 1 — Character + setting woven together
Zara had always been too tall for doorways and too loud for libraries — which made the reading room of St. Aldric's School for the Gifted particularly ironic. Dust motes drifted through amber light, the smell of old paper hung in the air like a held breath, and Zara sat in the corner, knees folded to her chest, reading about dragons.
Example 2 — Setting first, then character
The harbour at Millhaven looked like a painting in the early morning — fishing boats rocking gently, the water a bruised purple, mist coiling off the surface like smoke from a dying fire. Into this quiet stepped twelve-year-old Eli, school bag slung over one shoulder, a map that shouldn't exist tucked carefully inside it.
Example 3 — Inside the character's head
Tom had three rules: don't draw attention, don't ask questions, and definitely don't go anywhere near the old observatory on the hill. He'd broken all three by lunchtime on his first day at Caldwell Academy, which was, he would later reflect, entirely on brand.

Rising Action
The problem builds — tension and trouble
0 words
▼
✨ Writing tips for rising action
  • Introduce the main problem or conflict — what goes wrong?
  • Show how your character reacts — thoughts, feelings, actions
  • Add obstacles — things that make solving the problem harder
  • Build suspense — slow down time during tense moments
  • Use your character's fears or weaknesses against them
  • Use short sentences to speed up the pace when needed
✦ 3 examples of great writing Click a word to copy it to your writing
Example 1 — Problem builds through discovery
It started with a name scratched into the underside of her desk. Then a locker that wasn't hers opened when she touched it. Then the photograph in the school archive — a girl who looked exactly like Maya, standing in the same corridor, wearing the same expression. Dated 1987. Maya hadn't been born until 2012.
Example 2 — Tension through obstacles
Every route to the lighthouse was blocked. The coast road: closed, orange barriers across it and a sign that simply read NO ENTRY. The cliff path: half-collapsed, chalk crumbling into the sea below. The beach: the tide was in, grey waves swallowing the sand completely. Finn looked at all three options and understood, slowly, that someone did not want him getting there.
Example 3 — Internal and external conflict
The map said three hours to the summit. Nadia had done it in two the year before. But that was before the snow, before her ankle had started to throb with every step, and before she'd realised — with a cold, slow horror — that the trail markers had stopped appearing.

Climax
The biggest, most powerful moment
0 words
▼
✨ Writing tips for your climax
  • This is the turning point — the moment everything changes
  • Slow WAY down — write every second in detail
  • Use all five senses — make the reader feel like they're there
  • Show your character making a crucial decision or taking action
  • Use short, punchy sentences for maximum impact
  • This should be your most vivid, emotional writing
✦ 3 examples of great writing Click a word to copy it to your writing
Example 1 — Sensory, slowed-down climax
The bridge groaned. A sound like a giant waking up — deep, metallic, wrong. Leo's fingers tightened on the rope. Below him, forty metres of empty air. Above him, Sasha's face, pale as paper, hand outstretched. He had exactly one second to decide. The rope held. His hand reached. Their fingers touched. The bridge fell.
Example 2 — Quiet but powerful climax
Amara put the letter down. She didn't cry. She'd expected this — she'd known, really, since March. But knowing something and reading it in black ink are different things. The kitchen was very still. Outside, a bird was singing. She thought: it's strange that the world just keeps going.
Example 3 — Action and emotion combined
She ran. Not the panicked stumbling of before — this time her feet found a rhythm, arms pumping, lungs burning in the best way. The door was ten metres away. Five. She hit it with her shoulder, felt it give, burst into blinding white daylight, and collapsed onto grass that smelled like rain and safety, gasping, laughing, alive.

Falling Action
After the peak — things begin to settle
0 words
▼
✨ Writing tips for falling action
  • Show the immediate aftermath of the climax
  • How has your character changed? What are they feeling?
  • Tie up loose ends — what happened to other characters?
  • Slow the pace back down — use longer, calmer sentences
  • Let the reader breathe before the ending

Example 1 — Aftermath and reflection
Nobody spoke on the drive home. Dad kept both hands on the wheel and stared straight ahead. Mum had her eyes closed, not sleeping — just somewhere else. Olu sat in the back and watched the streetlights slide past the window, each one a small orange bloom in the dark, and thought: I think we're going to be okay. I think.
Example 2 — Physical and emotional settling
The storm had passed. The kitchen ceiling was ruined — great dark patches spreading across the plaster like a map of somewhere — but the house was quiet and the rain had stopped. Jess swept up the broken tiles slowly, methodically, each scrape of the dustpan steady and deliberate. Outside, a pale strip of light appeared on the horizon.
Example 3 — Changed relationships
Marcus found Priya sitting on the steps outside, knees pulled up, staring at nothing. He sat down beside her without saying anything. After a while, she said: 'I didn't think it would work.' He said: 'Neither did I.' They sat there for a long time after that, which was, somehow, more than enough.

Resolution
How it ends — what your character learns
0 words
▼
✨ Writing tips for your resolution
  • How has your character grown or changed from the start?
  • End with something that feels earned — not rushed
  • Consider echoing something from your hook (a full-circle ending)
  • Leave the reader with a feeling — hope, wonder, satisfaction, or even a twist
  • A great last line is worth spending time on
✦ 3 examples of great writing Click a word to copy it to your writing
Example 1 — Full circle ending
A year later, Maya passed the same street corner where it had all started. The door was still there — she'd half-expected it to be gone. She stopped, looked at it, and smiled. Then she walked on. Some doors, she'd learned, you don't need to go through twice.
Example 2 — Character has changed
The old Eli would have kept the discovery to himself, buried it deep, pretended it never happened. But that boy had been left somewhere on the harbour road, sometime in October, in the rain. This Eli walked into the police station, map in hand, and asked to speak to someone about the missing lighthouse keeper.
Example 3 — Quiet, resonant ending
Tom never did find out what was inside the observatory. He told himself that was fine. Some mysteries weren't meant to be solved — they were just meant to make you braver for having asked the question. He kept the key anyway, in the front pocket of his school bag, cold and heavy and his.
